Artificial Intelligence (AI)

Artificial intelligence is the ability of a computer or computer-controlled
robot to perform tasks that are commonly associated with the intellectual
processes characteristic of humans, such as the ability to reason. Although
there are as of yet no AIs that match full human flexibility over wider domains
or in tasks requiring much everyday knowledge, some AIs perform specific tasks
as well as humans.

Methods and goals in AI

AI research follows two distinct, and to some extent competing, methods, the
symbolic (or “top-down”) approach and the connectionist (or “bottom-up”) approach.
The top-down approach seeks to replicate intelligence by analyzing cognition
independent of the biological structure of the brain, in terms of the processing
of symbols—whence the symbolic label. The bottom-up approach, on the other hand,
involves creating artificial neural networks in imitation of the brain’s
structure—whence the connectionist label.

A Brief History of AI

The idea of creating intelligent machines is very old, appearing in ancient myths
about artificial life and robots. In modern times, the concept of AI began to take
shape with pioneers like Alan Turing, who developed the "Turing test" to see if
computers could think. John McCarthy, often called the "Father of Artificial
Intelligence," coined the term and worked on creating intelligent machines and
programming languages for AI.
